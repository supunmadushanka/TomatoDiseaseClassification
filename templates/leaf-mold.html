Tomato Leaf Mold

Introduction

Tomato leaf mold is a fungal disease caused by the pathogen Fulvia fulva (formerly known as Cladosporium fulvum). It is a common disease in tomato plants, particularly in areas with high humidity and moderate temperatures. Leaf mold primarily affects the foliage of tomato plants, causing characteristic yellowish-brown lesions on the leaves. The disease can lead to defoliation and reduced yield if not managed properly. Control measures for tomato leaf mold include the use of resistant tomato varieties, proper plant spacing, good sanitation practices, and fungicides.


Identification
  • Symptoms of tomato leaf mold include yellowish-brown lesions that develop on the upper side of the leaves
  • The lesions may have a fuzzy or velvety texture, particularly on the underside of the leaves
  • Infected leaves may also exhibit chlorosis (yellowing) and curling
  • Unlike other tomato diseases, leaf mold does not typically affect the stem or fruit of the plant

Managing and Treatment
  • Use resistant tomato varieties that are specifically bred to be resistant to leaf mold
  • Maintain proper plant spacing to promote good air circulation and reduce humidity, which can inhibit fungal growth
  • Remove and destroy infected plant material to prevent the spread of the disease
  • Practice good sanitation by removing weeds, debris, and fallen leaves that can serve as a source of infection
  • Use fungicides to control leaf mold; follow label instructions and apply fungicides preventatively or at the first signs of infection
  • Apply fungicides that specifically target leaf mold and rotate different active ingredients to prevent the development of resistance
  • Consider using organic or biological fungicides as alternatives to synthetic chemicals, if available and appropriate for your farming practices
  • Regularly monitor plants for signs of infection and take action promptly to prevent the spread of the disease.
